  • Business value 1: Consistency connecting varied, cross-platform, and significantly different products.

  • Business value 2: Positioning of each of the tools in front of external competition, and within the offering

The AppSec realm:

a Pipeline of an application defense includes various testing methods, in order to spot and gather potential vulnerabilities. Often automated, those tests are made by different professionals at different phases along the Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC):

  • Static Scans (SAST)
  • Dynamic Scans (DAST) 
  • Software Composition Analysis (SCA) 
  • Penetration Tests
  • Interactive Application Security Testing (IAST)
  • Runtime Application Self-Protection (RASP)

Once a vulnerability is discovered, it is added to a list of security issues, labeled by its estimated level of severity, categorized, and prioritized for fixing through a triage process, often managed by a dedicated Application Security Orchestration and Correlation (ASOC) tool.

As security teams plan and build their application defenses each according to unique requirements and potential threats, often several tools from different brands will be at work in differnt spots over the pipeline, and the unique identity and positioning of each tool in front of its direct competition is as important as its affiliation with the brand offering.

Products identities

Basing on HCL AppScan technology as our point of departure, we designed a unique visual identity for each of its major tools in the offering, positioning it as product in its own right, while also basing its unmistakable affiliation with the parent brand.

Cross-Platform Design System

The most important aspect of the System in this case, was its "Negative space": the ability to contain product differentiations within the offering, while gradually building UI consistency through them. This was achieved by addressing the design framework in 3 areas:

Design principles:

Design principles provide a level of abstraction that help anchor design decisions in points that are common to the essence of the entire offering, rather than bias towards one tool or another.
In our case, two levels of principles were at work: The general level, addressing the entire offering ("Clarity", "Reliability", "Simplicity") and more specific principles for each product ("Only one main action in a view", "Guidance over choice" in one product and "provide maximum Affordance" in another), knowing that each general principle might present itself differently in different usecase. 

Content patterns consistency:

Content Patterns are the structures by which each unit of content or information is organized. Content unit could be:

  • an article
  • a code snippet
  • a Security issue description
  • an event notification

or any other content repeating itself across the systems. Keeping consistent patterns can improve content recognizability across the different tools of an offerings, even when they differ in styles and visibility.

Core pattern library:

Core Pattern Library, serving complete range of AppScan products, branching to Web, Windows applications and IDE plugins, allowing each platform to stay consistent while expanding as needed.
